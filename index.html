Design Philosophy

This game was made as part of a 72-hour game Jam with the theme of "Charm". I primarily handled the general story/combat design and programming the boss. When ideating the game, I wanted to put a spin on the classic bullet-hell boss by making the player have to defeat the enemy with charm and wit rather than through attacks. So, I immediately thought about the dragon-seducing bard stereotype. That's how Finley the Charming Bard came to be. Then, the gameplay loop evolved from there

As the fight progresses, the player has to constantly write poems while choosing words that spawn around the arena to complete the poem. Depending on the word they choose, they get certain powerups that will allow them to survive the dragon's onslaught until they can complete the next verse. I chose this design to give player's a sense of agency and choice, with the powerups giving players meaningful abilities depending on what they choose. Additionally, when designing the dragon's combat attacks, I tried to make it clear that the dragon was slowly getting charmed by the player. For example, the dragon's fireballs would eventually take on a heart shape as the battle goes on. An important feature we weren't able to implement due to time restraints is the freezing of the game when words spawn. This would allow players time to read the poem that has been writing in the background, which their attention would most likely be on dodging attacks as the game went on.

Medical Simulation

This was developed with the UF Shands Hospital in order to help surgeons perform the Caudal Block Procedure on babies. My work on this project was primarily technical. I helped link the physical needle and baby model to the ones in Unity, enabled an ultrasound feature that would allow users to see where their needle is inside of the baby, and added scoring features that told users if they inserted the needle at the right angle and into the correct depth.

The main thing I learned from this project was how to work with a team, since I had to constantly work with doctors, engineers, and other programmers to ensure medical accuracy with this project. It also taught me a lot about how to design something with end users in mind. Because the target audience for this project was young surgeons who were not necessarily used to this type of technology, we focused on keeping the UI simple and focusing on functionality and accuracy over all else.

User-Centered Development

I created this project with a small team of friends in order to make a better platform for UF engineering students to create their schedule. I primarily handled the backend and ideation for this project.

This project helped me learn about how to use an API and work with large amounts of data, since I had to parse through and utilize course information about everything that has been offered at UF. Additionally, it also taught me how to write efficient algorithms that could handle this level of data. However, the main thing I learned about creating GatorGuide was thinking from the end user's perspective. We really focused on what students like us would want out of a schedule planner, which is how we eventually settled on the features we would prioritize, such as a pre-requisite checker, semester planner, and my personal favorite, a running list of what you are missing from your four-year plan in order to graduate.

Learning & Iteration

Both of these games, while incredibly different, helped me learn similar lessons about the importance of constant iteration. While Turtle All The Way Up is a 2-D platformer made in Unity focused on a roguelike upgrade system and an interesting respawn system, Push and Pull Simulator was a prototype I made in Unreal Engine 5 that focuses on a single interesting mechanic: pushing and pulling yourself and objects.

However, both of these projects required extensive testing and iterating to get right. For Turtle All The Way Up, I had many friends playtest the game, which was vital for fine tuning the numbers, upgrades, and most importantly, ensuring all gameplay mechanics were understandable. Similarly, other people's feedback on Push and Pull Simulator helped me figure out the best VX to use in order to improve gameplay clarity. Both of these projects were valuable lessons in the importance of getting user feedback early and utilizing it along with your own judgement to make the best game possible.
